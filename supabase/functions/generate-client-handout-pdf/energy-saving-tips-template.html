Lighting — The Single Biggest Quick Win
Replace all halogen downlights with LEDs. A 5W LED produces the same light as a 50W halogen — that's 90% less energy. LEDs last 15,000-25,000 hours vs 2,000 for halogen. They also run cool, reducing fire risk in insulated ceilings Save £200+/yr
The maths: A kitchen with 8 × 50W halogens running 5 hours/day costs £210/year. Replace with 8 × 5W LEDs: £21/year. The LEDs cost £20-30 total and pay for themselves in under 3 weeks
Motion sensors (PIR) in hallways, bathrooms, utility rooms, and garages mean lights are only on when someone is there. A bathroom light left on 8 hours a day unnecessarily costs £12/year — for one room
Dimmer switches save energy and extend bulb life. At 75% brightness, you use 20% less power. Modern LED dimmers are smooth, silent, and cost £15-25 to install
Outdoor lighting: Use dusk-to-dawn sensors instead of manual switches or timers. Solar-powered garden lights cost nothing to run. LED security floodlights use 10-30W vs 500W for old halogen floods Save £50/yr
Heating & Hot Water — Where 60% of Your Bill Goes
Turn your thermostat down by 1°C. You genuinely won't notice the difference between 21°C and 20°C — but you'll save approximately £80/year. The World Health Organisation recommends 18°C as a healthy minimum Save £80/yr
Use your programmer properly. Set heating to come on 30 minutes before you wake and switch off 30 minutes before bed — rooms retain warmth from residual heat. Don't leave it on 24/7 "because it's cheaper" — that's a myth
Smart thermostats (Hive £150, Nest £200, Tado £150) learn your routine, detect when you leave the house via your phone's GPS, and adjust automatically. Independent studies show 10-15% savings — that's £100-150/year on an average heating bill Save £150/yr
Radiator valves (TRVs): Fit thermostatic radiator valves to control each room individually. Turn down spare bedrooms, hallways, and rooms you rarely use. A TRV costs £8-15 and fitting takes 10 minutes
Hot water cylinder: Set to exactly 60°C — higher wastes energy, lower risks legionella bacteria growth. Insulate the cylinder with an 80mm jacket (£15 from any DIY store, saves £35/year). If you have a combi boiler, you don't have a cylinder

Standby Power — The Silent Energy Drain
The average UK home wastes £60-80/year powering devices that are "off" but still drawing electricity. That red standby light costs real money
Switch off at the wall — TVs, games consoles, set-top boxes, soundbars, monitors, printers. If it has a remote control, it's using standby power
Smart plug strips (£15-25) let you switch off 4-6 devices with one tap on your phone, or set schedules. Put one behind the TV unit and one at the computer desk
Phone and laptop chargers draw power even with no device attached. A charger left plugged in 24/7 wastes £3-8/year — multiply by the number of chargers in your home

Kitchen — Where Most Energy Is Used
Kettle: Only boil what you need. A full kettle (1.7L) uses 0.13 kWh per boil. A single cup (0.3L) uses 0.03 kWh — 77% less. If you boil a full kettle 4 times daily, you're wasting £40/year boiling water you pour away
Washing machine: Wash at 30°C — modern detergents clean just as effectively. Each wash at 30°C vs 60°C uses 40% less energy. Also, always wait for a full load rather than running half-loads
Tumble dryer: The most expensive appliance to run — each cycle uses 3-5 kWh, costing £1.00-1.50 per load. That's £150-250/year if used daily. Use a washing line or indoor airer whenever possible. When you must use it, clean the lint filter every time — a blocked filter increases energy use by 25%
Fridge/freezer: Runs 24/7 — test the door seal by closing it on a £5 note. If it slides out easily, the seal needs replacing. Defrost when ice exceeds 5mm — ice build-up increases energy use by 30%. Use a slow cooker or air fryer instead of the oven for smaller meals — they use a fraction of the energy
Smart Home — Technology That Pays for Itself
Energy monitors (Loop £50, Hildebrand £30) clip onto your electricity meter and show real-time usage on your phone. Most people discover at least one "energy vampire" they didn't know about. Seeing your usage in real-time changes behaviour immediately Save £50-100/yr
Smart plugs (£10-15 each) — monitor individual appliance energy use, set schedules, create "away mode" routines. Put them on your most-used devices to understand what's actually costing you money
Smart thermostat: Already mentioned but worth repeating — a Hive, Nest, or Tado pays for itself within 12-18 months and then saves you money every year after that Save £100-150/yr
Immersion heater timer: If you have a hot water cylinder with an immersion heater, don't leave it on 24/7. A £15 time switch set to heat water for 1-2 hours morning and evening saves £50-80/year

Solar PV — Generate Free Electricity
How it works: Panels on your roof convert sunlight into electricity. A typical 4kW system (10 panels) generates 3,400-4,200 kWh/year — covering 60-80% of average household electricity
Cost & payback: A 4kW system costs £5,000-7,000 installed. At current electricity prices (28p/kWh), it pays for itself in 6-8 years, then generates free electricity for 20+ more years
Export payments: Electricity you don't use goes to the grid. Your supplier pays you via the Smart Export Guarantee — typically 4-15p/kWh. With a good tariff (Octopus Flux at 15p), export payments alone can reach £200-300/year
Orientation: South-facing at 30-40° is ideal, but east/west produces 80-85% as much. Even north-facing works for some properties. No planning permission needed for most homes

Battery Storage — Store Cheap Power for Later
With solar: Instead of exporting excess daytime solar at 5p/kWh, store it in a battery and use it in the evening when grid electricity costs 28p/kWh. You save the 23p/kWh difference on every unit stored
Without solar: Charge overnight on a cheap tariff like Octopus Go (7.5p/kWh) and use during the day (28p/kWh). A 5kWh battery cycling daily saves £350-500/year on the tariff difference alone
Cost: A 5kWh battery (GivEnergy, Fox ESS) costs £2,500-4,000 installed. Combined with the right tariff, payback is 5-7 years. A Tesla Powerwall (13.5kWh) costs £8,000-10,000 but stores more
