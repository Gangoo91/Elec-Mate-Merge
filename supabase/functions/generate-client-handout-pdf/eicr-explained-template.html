You have received an EICR (Electrical Installation Condition Report) from {{ company_name }}. This is a thorough inspection and test of your electrical installation — the wiring, sockets, switches, consumer unit, earthing, and protection devices. This guide explains what it all means in plain English.
What Was Inspected?
All wiring and cables throughout the property — checked for damage, deterioration, and correct sizing
Every socket, switch, and light fitting — tested for correct operation, polarity, and physical condition
Consumer unit (fuse board) — all MCBs, RCDs, and RCBOs tested for correct operation and trip times
Earthing and bonding — the primary safety system that protects you from electric shock. Connections checked to gas, water, and other services
Electrical tests — insulation resistance, earth fault loop impedance (Zs), continuity, and RCD trip times measured and compared against BS 7671 limits
Understanding the Classification Codes

Your electrician assigns a code to each observation found during the inspection. Here's what they mean:

C1
DANGER PRESENT
An immediate risk of injury exists. Your electrician will have already made this safe — typically by isolating the affected circuit. Urgent remedial work is required.
C2
POTENTIALLY DANGEROUS
Not immediately dangerous but could become so. Remedial work should be completed as soon as possible. Do not ignore C2 findings.
C3
IMPROVEMENT RECOMMENDED
Does not meet current standards but is not dangerous. Improvement is recommended but not mandatory. Consider addressing these if selling, remortgaging, or renting.
FI
FURTHER INVESTIGATION
More investigation is needed to determine the extent of a potential problem. This may require opening up floor or ceiling voids, or specialist testing equipment.
Satisfactory or Unsatisfactory?
SATISFACTORY
Your installation meets safety standards. No C1 or C2 defects were found. You may have C3 improvements recommended — these are advisory, not urgent. Your installation is safe for continued use.
UNSATISFACTORY
C1 or C2 defects were found. These need attention. Review the observations on your report and contact {{ company_name }} to arrange remedial work. For rented properties, landlords must act within 28 days.

If Your EICR Is Unsatisfactory
C1 defects: Your electrician should have already made these safe on site — typically by isolating the circuit or removing the danger. Confirm that the remedial work is booked to permanently fix the issue
C2 defects: Arrange for remedial work as soon as possible. These are not immediately dangerous but could become so. Do not put these off
C3 improvements: Not mandatory, but consider them — especially if you're selling, remortgaging, or renting the property. Insurers may also ask about C3 findings
FI items: Further investigation is needed. This may uncover additional issues. Your electrician will advise on what's involved and any additional costs
Landlords — Your Legal Obligations
Electrical Safety Standards in the Private Rented Sector (England) Regulations 2020
Since 1 April 2021, landlords must have an EICR carried out by a qualified electrician every 5 years. If the report is unsatisfactory, all C1 and C2 defects must be remedied within 28 days (or sooner if specified by the electrician). Failure to comply can result in fines of up to £30,000. You must provide a copy of the EICR to your tenants within 28 days, and to your local authority within 7 days if requested.
New tenancies: You must have a valid EICR before the tenancy begins and provide it to tenants before they move in
Existing tenancies: The EICR must be no more than 5 years old. If yours has expired, book a new one immediately
After remedial work: Your electrician must confirm in writing that the defects have been remedied. Keep this with your EICR
Records: Keep all EICRs for the life of the installation. You'll need them for insurance, property sales, and letting agent compliance checks
Understanding Your Report
Schedule of Inspections: Lists every item checked — sockets, switches, connections, earthing — with a result for each (satisfactory, C1, C2, C3, or N/A)
Schedule of Test Results: The electrical measurements for each circuit — insulation resistance, earth fault loop impedance (Zs), and RCD trip times. These are compared against BS 7671 limits
Observations: Detailed descriptions of every defect found, including its location, the applicable BS 7671 regulation, and the recommended remedial action
Overall assessment: The summary at the front of the report — satisfactory or unsatisfactory. This is the key outcome
Next inspection date: Your electrician will recommend when the next EICR is due — typically 5 years for domestic, 3 years for commercial
Common EICR Findings Explained
"Lack of RCD protection" — your sockets don't have the life-saving protection that prevents electric shock. This is a C2 or C3 depending on the circuit. A consumer unit upgrade will fix this
"No main bonding to gas/water" — a critical safety connection is missing. Without bonding, a fault on the gas or water pipes could give you an electric shock. Usually a C2
"Zs exceeds maximum permitted" — the earth fault protection on a circuit is too slow. If a fault occurred, the circuit breaker may not trip quickly enough. Usually a C2
"No SPD fitted" — no surge protection device. Required on all new consumer units since 2018. Protects your electronics from voltage spikes. Usually a C3
