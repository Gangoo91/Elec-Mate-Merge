Understanding Your Consumer Unit
Your consumer unit (also called a fuse board) is the heart of your electrical system. It distributes electricity to every circuit in your home and protects you from faults
MCBs (Miniature Circuit Breakers) protect individual circuits. Each one controls a different part of your home — lights, sockets, cooker, shower, etc. When a fault occurs, the MCB "trips" (switches off) to protect the wiring
RCDs (Residual Current Devices) are the wider switches, usually with a "T" or "Test" button. These protect YOU from electric shock. They detect tiny imbalances in the current and cut power in milliseconds — fast enough to save your life
RCBOs combine the protection of an MCB and an RCD in one device. If your board has RCBOs, a fault on one circuit won't affect any others — only the faulty circuit trips. This is the modern standard for new installations
SPD (Surge Protection Device) protects your home from voltage spikes caused by lightning or grid surges. Required in all new consumer units since 2018. It protects expensive electronics — TVs, computers, smart home devices — from damage
The main switch turns off ALL electricity to your home. Know where it is — you may need it in an emergency. It's usually the large switch at the top or side of the consumer unit
Keep your consumer unit accessible
Never block your consumer unit with furniture, storage, or coats. In an emergency, you need to reach it quickly. There should be at least 1 metre of clear space in front of it.
What's changing? — BS 7671 Amendment 4 (April 2026)
New regulations are being introduced for battery energy storage systems (home batteries like Tesla Powerwall, GivEnergy), smart home wiring (Power over Ethernet for lighting and building controls), and ICT equipment earthing. If you're planning a battery storage installation, solar PV expansion, or smart home upgrade, speak to {{ company_name }} about the latest requirements.
How to Test Your RCD Protection
1
Find the test button — look for switches on your consumer unit with a button marked "T" or "Test". You may have one or two RCDs
2
Press the test button firmly — the switch should trip immediately, cutting power to the circuits it protects. Some lights or sockets will go off — this is normal
3
Check it tripped — if the switch did NOT move, contact {{ company_name }} immediately on {{ company_phone }}. Your life-saving protection is not working
4
Reset the RCD — push the switch firmly back up to restore power
How often?
Test your RCDs every 3 months. It takes 30 seconds. Set a reminder on your phone — the 1st of January, April, July, and October is easy to remember. This simple test could save a life.

What to Do When Something Trips
A single MCB has tripped: This usually means a fault on one circuit. Unplug everything on that circuit, reset the MCB. Plug items back in one at a time. The one that causes the trip is faulty — stop using it
An RCD has tripped: This affects multiple circuits. Try resetting it. If it trips again immediately, there's a fault. Turn off all MCBs under that RCD, reset the RCD, then turn MCBs on one at a time to find which circuit has the fault
Everything has tripped: Check with your neighbours — it may be a power cut from the grid, not a fault in your home. If it's just you, try the main switch
It keeps tripping repeatedly: Do NOT keep resetting it. This indicates a serious fault. Call {{ company_name }} on {{ company_phone }}
Warning Signs — Call an Electrician Immediately
A burning smell from sockets, switches, or the consumer unit — this could indicate overheating or arcing
Brown or black marks around sockets or switches — signs of heat damage or electrical arcing
Sockets or switches that feel warm or hot to the touch — this is never normal and indicates a loose connection or overload
Sparking or crackling sounds from sockets, switches, or inside walls
Electric shocks or tingling when touching appliances, light switches, or metal surfaces
Lights that flicker constantly or dim noticeably when appliances switch on — occasional flicker with a high-power appliance (kettle, vacuum) can be normal, but persistent flickering is not
Frequent tripping of circuit breakers or RCDs — this means there is an active fault
If you notice any of these signs, switch off the affected circuit at the consumer unit and contact {{ company_name }} on {{ company_phone }}. Do not attempt to investigate or repair the fault yourself.
Socket & Plug Safety
One plug per socket — avoid multi-way adapters. If you need more sockets, have additional ones installed properly
Never daisy-chain extension leads — plugging one extension into another creates a fire risk
Check the rating — a standard extension lead is rated at 13A (3,120W). Don't plug in more than this combined

The 3,120W Rule
Never exceed 3,120 watts on a single socket or extension lead (13A × 240V). A kettle alone uses up to 3,000W — so never plug a kettle into an extension lead with other appliances.
Bathroom & Kitchen Safety
Bathrooms: No standard sockets allowed in bathrooms. Only shaver sockets (transformer-isolated) and pull-cord switches are permitted. Extractor fans, showers, and heated towel rails must be installed by a qualified electrician
Kitchens: Keep sockets away from the sink and hob. Water and electricity don't mix. Never use appliances with wet hands. Ensure worktop appliances are plugged into dedicated sockets, not extension leads
Outdoor areas: All outdoor sockets must have RCD protection. Don't use indoor extension leads outside. Use weatherproof equipment rated IP44 or higher. Never use a garden power tool in wet conditions
Cable & Appliance Safety
Never run cables under carpets or rugs — they overheat and create a fire risk. Use proper cable management (trunking, clips)
Replace any cable that is frayed, cracked, or has exposed wires immediately. Don't tape over damaged cables
Check appliance cables where they enter the plug — this is where damage most commonly occurs from pulling
Register your appliances at registermyappliance.org.uk — you'll be notified of safety recalls

Smoke, Heat & CO Alarms
Test all alarms monthly — press and hold the test button until you hear the alarm. If it doesn't sound, replace the battery or the unit
Replace batteries annually in battery-powered alarms. Replace the entire alarm unit every 10 years — the sensor degrades over time
Smoke alarms: Fit on every floor, ideally in hallways and landings. Do NOT put them in kitchens or bathrooms (steam causes false alarms)
Heat alarms: Use in kitchens instead of smoke alarms — they respond to temperature, not smoke, so no false alarms from cooking
Carbon monoxide (CO) alarms: Required in any room with a gas appliance, oil boiler, or solid fuel burner. CO is odourless and kills — you CANNOT detect it without an alarm
What do the beeps mean?
Continuous loud alarm: Danger detected — evacuate and call 999. Intermittent chirping (every 30-60 seconds): Low battery — replace immediately. Three beeps then silence: End of life — replace the entire unit.
Outdoor Electrics
Garden lighting: Low-voltage (12V) garden lights are safest for DIY installation. Mains voltage (240V) garden lighting must be installed by a qualified electrician with proper outdoor-rated cable and RCD protection
Outdoor sockets: Must be protected by a 30mA RCD and have a weatherproof enclosure (IP66). Don't use indoor multi-socket adapters outside
Hot tubs & pools: Must be on a dedicated circuit with RCD protection, installed by a qualified electrician. Never use standard extension leads to power a hot tub
Sheds & outbuildings: If you want mains power in a shed, it must be installed by a qualified electrician using proper armoured cable (SWA). Do not run extension leads permanently to outbuildings
Lawn mowers & power tools: Always use an RCD adapter (available from any DIY store, ~£15) when using power tools outdoors. If you cut through the cable, the RCD will save your life
Children's Electrical Safety
Socket covers are NOT recommended — UK sockets have built-in shutters that only open when a proper plug is inserted. Cheap socket covers can actually defeat the shutter mechanism and make sockets LESS safe. This is the official advice from the IET and Electrical Safety First
Teach children that electricity is dangerous. They should never poke anything into sockets, play with plugs, or touch switches with wet hands
Keep cables tidy — children can pull on hanging cables and bring appliances (kettles, irons, lamps) down on themselves
Phone and tablet chargers: Don't leave chargers plugged in within children's reach. The USB end can be inserted into mouths. Unplug when not in use
The socket cover myth
The Electrical Safety Council (now Electrical Safety First) and the IET have both confirmed that UK 13A sockets do not need and should not have socket covers. The built-in shutters provide adequate protection. Socket covers sold in shops can actually compromise the safety mechanism.
Christmas & Seasonal Safety
Check Christmas lights for damage before each use — replace any with cracked holders, bare wires, or missing bulbs
Use LED lights — they run cool, use less power, and are much safer than older filament types
Outdoor lights must be rated for outdoor use (check for IP44+ rating). Use an RCD adapter for outdoor decorations
Turn off all decorative lights when you go to bed or leave the house. Use a timer to do this automatically

In an Emergency
Electric Shock
Do NOT touch the person — you will also receive a shock. Switch off the supply at the consumer unit first. If you cannot reach the switch safely, use a dry non-metallic object (wooden broom handle, dry towel wrapped around your hand) to separate them from the source. Call 999 immediately. Begin CPR if the person is not breathing and you are trained to do so.
Electrical Fire
NEVER use water on an electrical fire — water conducts electricity. Unplug the appliance ONLY if it is safe to do so. Use a CO2 or dry powder fire extinguisher. If the fire is spreading, get out, close doors behind you, and call 999. Do not go back in.
Power Cut
Check your neighbours — if they're also out, it's a network fault. Call your Distribution Network Operator (DNO) on 105 (free, 24/7). If it's just your property: check your consumer unit for tripped switches. Try resetting. If it keeps tripping, call your electrician.
Electrical Inspections — EICR
An EICR (Electrical Installation Condition Report) is a thorough inspection and test of all the wiring, sockets, switches, and protection in your home
Rented properties: Landlords must have an EICR every 5 years — this is the law since April 2021 (Electrical Safety Standards Regulations)
Owner-occupied: Recommended every 10 years, or when buying/selling a property
After major work: Always get an EICR after a rewire, extension, or major renovation
Older properties: If your home was built before 1990 or has not been inspected, book an EICR. Old wiring deteriorates and can become dangerous
What does an EICR cost?
A typical domestic EICR costs £150-300 depending on the size of the property. It takes 2-4 hours. The electrician will need access to all rooms, the consumer unit, and the meter. It's a small price for the peace of mind that your home is electrically safe.
What You CAN Do Yourself (Safely)
Change a light bulb — switch off the light first, let the bulb cool, replace like-for-like
Change a fuse in a plug — always replace with the correct rating (3A for lamps/electronics, 13A for heating appliances)
Wire a plug — Brown to Live (right), Blue to Neutral (left), Green/Yellow to Earth (top)
Everything else requires a qualified electrician — replacing sockets or switches, new circuits, additional sockets, bathroom/kitchen work, consumer unit changes, outdoor wiring, EV charger installation
