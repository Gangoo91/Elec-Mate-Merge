This guide from {{ company_name }} explains how to get the most from your home EV charger — how to charge smartly, save money, maintain your equipment, and troubleshoot common issues. Home charging is the cheapest and most convenient way to fuel your electric vehicle.
How Home Charging Works
Your home charger (also called a wallbox or EVSE) converts your home's AC electricity to a form your car's battery can accept. It communicates with your vehicle to manage the charge safely
Type 2 connector is the UK standard for home and public AC charging. Your charger has either a tethered cable (permanently attached) or a socket where you plug in your own cable
Charging speed: A 7kW charger adds approximately 25-30 miles of range per hour. A typical EV (60kWh battery) charges from 20% to 80% in about 5-6 hours — perfect for overnight charging
Smart charging is required by law on all chargers installed since June 2022. Your charger connects to WiFi and can schedule charging for the cheapest electricity rates automatically

Understanding Your Charger
LED indicators: Most chargers use coloured lights — green = standby/ready, blue = charging in progress, flashing = scheduled/waiting for cheap rate, red = fault. Check your manufacturer's guide for exact meanings
Load management: If fitted, your charger automatically reduces power when your home's electricity demand is high (e.g. oven + shower running). This prevents your main fuse from tripping
WiFi connection: Keep your charger connected to WiFi for software updates, smart tariff integration, and remote monitoring. If your charger loses WiFi, it will still charge but without smart features
RCD protection: Your charger has dedicated RCD/RCBO protection in the consumer unit. If it trips, do not keep resetting — contact {{ company_name }}

Maintaining Your Charger
Keep the connector clean and dry. Wipe the plug and socket with a dry cloth regularly. If debris gets inside the connector, it can cause charging errors or reduced speed
Cable management: Don't leave the cable lying on the ground where it can be driven over, stepped on, or damaged by garden equipment. Use the cable holder on your charger unit. Coil loosely — tight coils generate heat
Check the cable regularly for cuts, cracks, or exposed wires — especially near the plug where flexing occurs. If damaged, stop using it immediately and contact us for a replacement
Software updates: Your charger receives updates over WiFi. Keep it connected. Updates may improve charging speed, add new tariff integrations, or fix bugs. Most update automatically overnight
Winter care: Your charger is weatherproof (IP65 rated) and works in all UK weather. In freezing conditions, the connector may be stiff — don't force it. Warm the plug in your hands for a moment first
Annual inspection: We recommend a visual inspection annually as part of your general electrical maintenance. Check for water ingress, loose mounting, and cable condition
Troubleshooting — Common Issues
Charger not starting: 80% of "not charging" reports are fixed in under a minute. First, unplug and firmly re-seat the connector — a loose connection is the most common cause. Then check your car isn't on a scheduled charge (timer set in car app). Finally, check the consumer unit for a tripped RCBO
Charging slower than expected: Check your car's charge setting — some vehicles default to a lower current (e.g. 10A instead of 32A). Cold weather also reduces charge speed as the battery management system protects the cells. Pre-conditioning helps
Red light / error indicator: Power cycle the charger — switch off at the consumer unit, wait 30 seconds, switch back on. If the error persists, note the light pattern and contact {{ company_name }}
RCBO keeps tripping: This indicates a ground fault — either in the charger or the vehicle. Try a different charging cable if you have a socket type. Do not keep resetting — contact {{ company_name }} on {{ company_phone }}
App not connecting: Check your WiFi signal at the charger location. If weak, consider a WiFi extender. Reset the charger's WiFi connection via the charger's setup button (consult manual)
Never attempt to open or repair the charger
Your charger contains high-voltage components. If you notice physical damage, burning smell, unusual sounds, or persistent errors, stop using it immediately, switch off at the consumer unit, and contact {{ company_name }}. Do not attempt DIY repairs.
Cold Weather & Battery Care
Range drops in winter — typically 15-25% less range in cold weather. This is normal and not a fault. The battery chemistry is less efficient at low temperatures, and cabin heating uses energy
Pre-condition while plugged in: Set your car to warm the cabin and battery before you unplug in the morning. This uses mains power (free via your charger) instead of battery power, maximising your range
Keep the battery between 20-80% for daily use. Deep discharges (below 10%) and constant full charges (100%) degrade the battery faster over time
Charge immediately after driving in cold weather — the battery is warmer from use and will accept charge faster than a cold-soaked battery that's been parked all day

Government Grants (OZEV) — April 2026 Update
EV Chargepoint Grant: From April 2026, renters, flat owners, and businesses can claim up to £500 per socket (increased from £350) or 75% of the cost, whichever is lower
Homeowners in detached/semi-detached houses are no longer eligible for the grant (removed in 2022). However, the investment pays for itself within 2-3 years through fuel savings
Landlords: Can claim the grant for rental properties — up to £500 per socket. Must use an OZEV-approved installer. The charger must have smart functionality
Workplace charging: Businesses can claim up to £500 per socket (max 40 sockets) for staff and visitor charging. Great for company car schemes

Solar PV + EV = Free Fuel
The ultimate combination: Solar panels generate free electricity during the day. A battery stores the excess. Your car charges overnight from the battery. Total fuel cost: virtually zero
Solar diverter: Some smart chargers (like the Zappi) can automatically use excess solar generation to charge your car instead of exporting to the grid at 5p/kWh. You save the full 28p/kWh difference
Vehicle-to-Grid (V2G): Emerging technology that allows your car to power your home during peak hours and recharge from cheap overnight electricity. Currently available on some Nissan and Hyundai models
Battery storage: A home battery (Tesla Powerwall, GivEnergy, Fox ESS) stores cheap overnight electricity or solar energy for evening use. Combined with an EV charger, you can run your entire home and car on cheap or free electricity
Public Charging — When You're Away From Home
Apps to download: Zap-Map (find chargers), Octopus Electroverse (one card for multiple networks), BP Pulse, Tesla Supercharger (now open to all). Most accept contactless payment
Rapid charging: 50-350kW DC chargers at motorway services can add 100+ miles in 20-30 minutes. Costs 60-80p/kWh — expensive compared to home charging. Use for long journeys only
Destination charging: Many hotels, supermarkets, and car parks offer free or cheap 7-22kW charging while you shop or stay. Check Zap-Map for availability
Route planning: Use A Better Route Planner (ABRP) for long journeys — it calculates charging stops based on your car's real-world range, weather, speed, and elevation
