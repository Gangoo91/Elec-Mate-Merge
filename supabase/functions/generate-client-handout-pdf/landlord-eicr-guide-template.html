What the Law Requires
Have an EICR every 5 years — carried out by a qualified and competent electrician. The report must cover the fixed electrical installation (wiring, sockets, consumer unit, earthing) — not portable appliances
New tenancies: You must have a valid EICR before the tenancy begins. The report must be no more than 5 years old at the start of the tenancy
Provide a copy to tenants within 28 days of the inspection, or before they move in for new tenancies
Provide to your local authority within 7 days if they request it
If unsatisfactory: Complete all C1 and C2 remedial work within 28 days of the report (or sooner if the electrician specifies)
After remedial work: Obtain written confirmation from the electrician that defects have been remedied. Provide this to tenants within 28 days of completion
The 28-Day Timeline
Day 0 — EICR Inspection
Your electrician inspects and tests the installation. You receive the EICR report.
Within 28 Days — Provide to Tenants
Give a copy of the EICR to every existing tenant. For new tenants, provide before move-in.
Within 28 Days — Complete Remedial Work
If unsatisfactory, all C1 and C2 defects must be fixed. Your electrician may specify a shorter deadline for C1 (danger present).
Within 28 Days of Remedial — Confirm to Tenants
Obtain written confirmation from your electrician. Provide to tenants and local authority if requested.
What Happens If You Don't Comply
Financial penalties
Your local authority can impose a fine of up to £30,000 per offence. Multiple breaches (e.g. no EICR + not providing to tenant + not completing remedial work) can each attract a separate fine. These are civil penalties — no court conviction needed.
Local authority intervention
If you fail to complete remedial work within 28 days, the local authority has the power to enter your property and arrange for the work to be done. They will recover all costs from you, payable within 21 days of the demand.

Understanding Your EICR Results
C1 — Danger Present: An immediate risk of injury. Your electrician should make this safe on site (usually by isolating the circuit). You must arrange permanent remedial work urgently — do not wait 28 days for C1 defects
C2 — Potentially Dangerous: Not immediately dangerous but could become so. Must be remedied within 28 days. Common C2 findings: lack of RCD protection, missing bonding, high Zs values
C3 — Improvement Recommended: Not dangerous, not legally required to fix. However, addressing C3 findings protects your property and tenants, and demonstrates duty of care if there's ever a dispute
FI — Further Investigation: Additional investigation needed. May uncover C1 or C2 defects. Budget for this — don't leave FI items uninvestigated
Best Practice — Go Beyond the Minimum
Fix C3 defects too. While not legally required, addressing C3 findings (like upgrading to RCD protection or fitting SPDs) protects your tenants and your investment. It also shows due diligence if there's ever an insurance claim or legal dispute
Don't wait until the last minute. Book your EICR 2-3 months before the previous one expires. This gives time for any remedial work without breaching the 5-year deadline
Use the same electrician. Continuity means they know the installation history, can spot deterioration, and provide more accurate reports. They also hold the previous test results for comparison
Keep a property compliance file. Store your EICR, gas safety certificate, EPC, fire risk assessment, and landlord insurance together. Some letting agents and insurers now require sight of a valid EICR
Inform tenants in advance. Give at least 24 hours written notice before the inspection. The electrician needs access to every room, the consumer unit, and the meter. An uncooperative tenant can delay your compliance
Common Mistakes Landlords Make
Thinking an EPC replaces an EICR. They are completely different documents. An EPC assesses energy efficiency. An EICR assesses electrical safety. You need both
Using an unqualified person. The regulations require a "qualified and competent person." Look for NICEIC, NAPIT, or ELECSA registration. A general builder or handyman cannot carry out an EICR
Ignoring FI (Further Investigation) items. An FI could be hiding a C1 or C2 defect behind a wall or ceiling. If you don't investigate and a tenant is injured, you have failed in your duty of care
Not keeping records. You must keep EICRs for the life of the installation. If a local authority asks for your EICR and you can't produce it within 7 days, you're in breach
Assuming old wiring is fine. Many rental properties have wiring from the 1960s-80s. Just because "nothing has gone wrong" doesn't mean it's safe. Deterioration happens invisibly inside walls
What Does an EICR Cost?
A domestic EICR typically costs £150-300 depending on the size of the property and number of circuits. It takes 2-4 hours. Remedial work costs vary — a consumer unit upgrade (the most common C2 fix) is typically £400-800. Compare this to a £30,000 fine for non-compliance, and the cost of an EICR is negligible.

HMOs — Additional Requirements
Mandatory HMO licensing (5+ tenants, 2+ households) requires a valid EICR as part of the licence application. Without one, your licence can be refused or revoked
Fire safety: HMOs have additional requirements for fire detection, emergency lighting, fire doors, and escape routes. An EICR alone does not cover fire safety — you need a separate fire risk assessment
PAT testing: While not a legal requirement for standard rentals, many HMO licensing conditions require portable appliance testing of any electrical equipment you provide (kettles, toasters, microwaves in communal areas)
Selective licensing: Some local authorities operate selective licensing schemes that require additional compliance beyond the national regulations. Check with your local council
Smoke & CO Alarms — Separate Legal Requirement
The Smoke and Carbon Monoxide Alarm (Amendment) Regulations 2022
Since October 2022, landlords must install smoke alarms on every storey used as living accommodation, heat alarms in kitchens, and carbon monoxide alarms in any room with a fixed combustion appliance (gas boiler, fire, etc.). You must ensure alarms are working at the start of each new tenancy. This is separate from your EICR obligation.
Insurance & Liability
Landlord insurance: Many insurers now require a valid EICR. If you make a claim and don't have one, your insurer may refuse to pay — especially for electrical fires or shock injuries
Liability: If a tenant is injured by an electrical fault and you don't have a current EICR, you are likely liable. A valid EICR demonstrates you took reasonable steps to ensure safety
Tax deductible: The cost of an EICR and any remedial work is an allowable expense against rental income for income tax purposes. Keep your invoices
Managing Agents — Who Is Responsible?
The legal responsibility always sits with the landlord, even if you use a managing agent. If your agent fails to arrange an EICR, you are liable — not the agent. Ensure your management agreement explicitly states that the agent is responsible for arranging EICRs and remedial work. Check they are actually doing it. Ask for copies of the reports.
Tenant Changeover Checklist
Check EICR validity — is the report less than 5 years old? If not, book a new one before the new tenancy starts
Check for outstanding defects — were all C1 and C2 defects from the last EICR remedied? Do you have written confirmation?
Test smoke, heat and CO alarms — replace batteries, replace units over 10 years old, ensure they all sound when tested
Visual inspection — check all sockets, switches, and light fittings for damage. Check for signs of DIY electrical work by the previous tenant
Provide documents — give the new tenant copies of the EICR, gas safety certificate, EPC, and how-to-rent guide before they move in
Show them the consumer unit — explain where it is, how to reset tripped switches, and how to test the RCDs
Scotland, Wales & Northern Ireland
Scotland: The Housing (Scotland) Act 2006 requires landlords to ensure electrical installations are safe, but 5-yearly EICRs are not yet a statutory requirement — they are strongly recommended by the Scottish Government. Wales: The Renting Homes (Wales) Act 2016 requires EICRs every 5 years, with similar obligations to England. Northern Ireland: No specific EICR requirement yet, but landlords have a general duty of care under common law. Check current local regulations as these are subject to change.
