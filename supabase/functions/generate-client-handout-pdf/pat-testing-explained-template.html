{{ company_name }} has carried out Portable Appliance Testing (PAT) on your electrical equipment in accordance with the IET Code of Practice for In-Service Inspection and Testing of Electrical Equipment (5th Edition). This guide explains what was tested, what the results mean, what the labels on your equipment mean, and what you need to do next.
What Is PAT Testing?
PAT testing is the inspection and testing of portable electrical equipment to ensure it is safe to use. It covers anything with a plug — from kettles and laptops to power tools and industrial machinery
It is NOT a single test. It is a structured process: first a visual inspection (checking the plug, cable, and casing for damage), then electrical tests using calibrated instruments (earth continuity, insulation resistance, and leakage current)
Legal basis: The Electricity at Work Regulations 1989 require duty holders to maintain electrical equipment in a safe condition. PAT testing is the established method of demonstrating compliance
Risk-based approach: The IET Code of Practice 5th Edition (2020) emphasises that testing frequency should be based on risk — not a one-size-fits-all annual schedule. The type of equipment, how it's used, and the environment determine how often it needs testing
Equipment Classes — What's the Difference?
I
Class I — Earthed Equipment
Has an earth connection (3-pin plug, metal casing). If a fault occurs, the earth provides a safe path for current. Examples: kettles, irons, washing machines, fridges, microwaves, toasters, most power tools
II
Class II — Double Insulated
Has two layers of insulation (no earth needed). Often has a square-within-a-square symbol. Examples: phone chargers, laptop chargers, hair dryers, some power tools, desk lamps, radios
III
Class III — Extra-Low Voltage (SELV)
Operates below 50V via a separate transformer or power supply. Inherently safe due to low voltage. Examples: USB devices, some LED lighting, battery chargers, doorbells
Why does the class matter?
Different classes require different tests. Class I appliances need earth continuity, insulation resistance, and leakage tests. Class II appliances don't have an earth, so only insulation resistance and leakage tests apply. Class III appliances typically only need a visual inspection.
What Tests Were Performed?
Visual inspection: The plug, cable, casing, and connections were checked for damage, wear, correct fuse rating, and signs of overheating. Over 90% of faults are found during the visual inspection alone
Earth continuity (Class I only): Confirms the earth conductor is intact from the plug earth pin to any accessible metal parts. A low resistance reading (typically below 0.1Ω for short leads) confirms a good earth connection
Insulation resistance: Tests the insulation between live/neutral conductors and earth. A high reading means the insulation is intact. Minimum: 1.0MΩ for Class I, 2.0MΩ for Class II
Protective conductor current (leakage): Measures how much current leaks to earth during normal operation. Maximum: 5mA for all classes. High leakage indicates insulation breakdown
Functional check: The appliance was switched on to confirm it operates correctly and safely — no unusual sounds, smells, or excessive heat

Understanding the Labels on Your Equipment
Green "PASS" label: The appliance passed all visual and electrical tests at the time of testing and is safe for continued use. The label shows the test date, next retest date, asset number, and the tester's details
Red "FAIL" / "DO NOT USE" label: The appliance failed one or more tests. It must be immediately removed from service. Do not use it, do not remove the label, and do not attempt to repair it yourself. It should be repaired by a competent person or disposed of safely
Asset number / barcode: Each appliance is given a unique asset number that links to its full test history in our digital records. This allows us to track every appliance's condition over time and identify deteriorating equipment before it becomes dangerous
Retest date: The label shows when the next test is due. This is based on the risk assessment for that specific type of equipment in your environment — not a generic annual date
Failed appliance? Do not use it
If an appliance has a red FAIL label, it has been identified as potentially dangerous. Using a failed appliance puts employees and visitors at risk and could result in prosecution under the Electricity at Work Regulations 1989. Remove it from service immediately.

What Your PAT Testing Certificate Shows
Asset register: A complete list of every appliance tested, including description, location, asset number, class, and serial number where visible
Test results: For each appliance — visual inspection outcome, earth continuity (Ω), insulation resistance (MΩ), leakage (mA), and overall PASS/FAIL result
Failed appliances: Listed separately with the reason for failure and recommended action (repair, replace, or dispose)
Next test date: The recommended date for the next round of testing based on the risk assessment
Tester details: Name, qualifications, and contact details of the person who carried out the testing

Your Legal Responsibilities
Electricity at Work Regulations 1989: As the duty holder (employer, landlord, or person in control of premises), you must ensure all electrical equipment is maintained in a safe condition to prevent danger. PAT testing is the established way to demonstrate this
Health and Safety at Work Act 1974: You have a general duty to ensure the health, safety, and welfare of employees and anyone affected by your undertaking — including visitors, contractors, and members of the public
Provision and Use of Work Equipment Regulations 1998 (PUWER): Work equipment must be maintained in an efficient state, in efficient working order, and in good repair. This includes portable electrical equipment
Record keeping: Keep your PAT testing certificate and asset register for at least the life of the equipment. Insurers, HSE inspectors, and local authorities may request to see them
Insurance: Most employers' liability and public liability policies require electrical equipment to be regularly inspected and tested. Failure to maintain PAT records could invalidate a claim
User Checks — What Staff Should Do Daily
Every employee should visually check equipment before each use
✓ Is the plug cracked, scorched, or damaged?
✓ Is the cable frayed, cut, or taped up?
✓ Is the outer sheath of the cable secure where it enters the plug and the appliance?
✓ Is the casing damaged, cracked, or showing burn marks?
✓ Is the equipment sitting in water or being used with wet hands?
✓ Does the PAT label show the appliance is within its test date?
✓ Does the equipment smell of burning, make unusual sounds, or get excessively hot?
If any check fails — stop using the appliance
Remove it from service, attach a "DO NOT USE" label or note, and report it to your manager or facilities team. Never attempt to repair electrical equipment unless you are competent to do so.
Common Causes of Appliance Failure
Cable damage — the most common failure. Caused by dragging across sharp edges, trapping in doors, running over with wheels, or pulling the cable instead of the plug to disconnect
Wrong fuse: A 13A fuse fitted where a 3A fuse is required means the fuse won't blow during a fault — the cable overheats instead. Chargers, lamps, and electronics should have 3A fuses. Heaters, kettles, and irons need 13A
Water ingress: Equipment used in kitchens, bathrooms, or outdoors is exposed to moisture. Even small amounts of water inside an appliance can cause insulation breakdown and electric shock
Overheating: Blocked vents, dusty fans, and running equipment beyond its duty cycle. Common with heaters, hair dryers, and power tools used continuously
Counterfeit equipment: Cheap imported appliances and chargers that don't meet UK safety standards. The IET 5th Edition specifically highlights this risk. Only buy from reputable suppliers and check for the UKCA or CE mark
New Equipment & Personal Items
New equipment: Brand new appliances from reputable suppliers do not need PAT testing immediately but should be visually inspected and added to your asset register before first use. Formal testing should begin at the next scheduled cycle
Personal items brought to work: Employee-owned phone chargers, fans, heaters, and radios are the duty holder's responsibility while on your premises. They should be included in your PAT testing programme. Consider a policy requiring staff to register personal electrical items
Extension leads: The most commonly failed items. They get damaged, overloaded, and used in unsuitable environments. Include them in testing and replace at the first sign of damage
