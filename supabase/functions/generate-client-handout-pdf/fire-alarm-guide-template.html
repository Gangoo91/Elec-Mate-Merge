This guide explains how your system works, what the different signals mean, your legal responsibilities for testing and maintenance, and what to do when the alarm activates. Share this with all staff and display it near your fire alarm panel.
How Your System Works
Fire alarm panel: The central control unit — usually located near the main entrance. It shows the status of every zone and detector, displays faults, and controls the alarm sounders. It should be accessible 24/7
Detectors: Smoke detectors (optical and ionisation), heat detectors (fixed temperature and rate-of-rise), and multi-sensor detectors are positioned throughout the building based on the fire risk assessment and system category
Manual call points (break glass): Red boxes at exits and on escape routes. Breaking the glass triggers the alarm for the entire building immediately. They are your manual backup if detectors haven't activated
Sounders and beacons: Must achieve minimum 65dB(A) or 5dB above background noise in all occupied areas. In sleeping areas: minimum 75dB(A) at the bed-head with all doors closed. Visual beacons are required where ambient noise is high or for hearing-impaired occupants
Zones: Your building is divided into zones so the panel shows exactly where the alarm has been triggered. The zone chart (displayed near the panel) maps zone numbers to locations
System Categories — What's Installed
L1
Full Coverage — Life Protection
Detectors in every room, space, and void. The highest level of protection. Required for care homes, hotels, and sleeping accommodation
L2
Extended Coverage — Escape Routes + High-Risk Areas
Escape routes + rooms opening onto them + high-risk areas. BS 5839-1:2025 now requires sleeping risk rooms to be included. Common in HMOs, schools, offices
L3
Standard Coverage — Escape Routes
Detectors on escape routes (hallways, corridors, stairwells) to give early warning while people can still escape. Minimum for most domestic properties
P1/P2
Property Protection
P1 = full coverage for property protection. P2 = coverage in defined high-risk areas only. Typically required by insurers for commercial premises
M
Manual Only — Call Points Only
Manual call points (break glass) on escape routes only. No automatic detection. Relies on people discovering the fire and activating manually
Understanding the Panel Signals
Green "Power" light on, no other lights: System normal — all zones healthy, mains power on, standby batteries charged. This is the state you want to see
Red "Fire" light + continuous alarm: A detector or call point has been activated. The panel display shows which zone. Follow your fire evacuation procedure immediately
Amber "Fault" light + intermittent beep: A fault has been detected — broken wiring, contaminated detector, low battery, or communication failure. The system may still detect fire but reliability is reduced. Report to {{ company_name }} immediately
"Disabled" or "Isolated" light: One or more zones or devices have been manually disabled. This means part of your building has no fire detection. This should only be temporary and must be logged. Never leave zones isolated overnight
"Test" light: The system is in test mode — sounders are silenced during weekly testing. The panel will exit test mode automatically after a set period

BS 5839-1:2025 Update
The latest standard (effective 2025) allows a service window of 5-7 months between inspections (previously strict 6 months). Servicing within this window is considered compliant. However, servicing more than 7 months after the last visit is non-compliant.
How to Conduct the Weekly Test
Step 1: Notify everyone in the building that a fire alarm test is about to take place — this prevents unnecessary evacuation
Step 2: If your system connects to a monitoring centre (ARC), call them first to put the system on test. This prevents a false call-out
Step 3: Use a different manual call point (break glass) each week. Use the test key to activate it — you don't actually break the glass during tests
Step 4: Confirm all sounders and beacons operate throughout the building. Walk the building if needed
Step 5: Reset the panel. Reset the call point using the test key. Confirm the panel shows normal (green light only)
Step 6: Record the test in your fire alarm log book — date, time, which call point was tested, result, who conducted the test. This is a legal requirement
The Fire Log Book — Your Legal Record
You must keep a fire log book. It is a legal requirement under the Regulatory Reform (Fire Safety) Order 2005. The fire service will ask to see it during any inspection
Record all weekly tests, false alarms, genuine activations, faults reported, zones isolated, maintenance visits, and fire drills. Include date, time, details, and who was responsible
Keep service records from {{ company_name }} in the log book. We provide a written report after every service visit detailing what was tested, any faults found, and recommendations
False alarms: Record every false alarm with the cause if known. Repeated false alarms from the same detector may indicate contamination, incorrect positioning, or environmental factors — report to us for investigation
Reducing False Alarms
Cooking: Kitchens should have heat detectors, not smoke detectors. If your kitchen has a smoke detector and it keeps false-alarming, contact {{ company_name }} to replace it with a heat detector
Steam: Bathrooms, shower rooms, and laundries — steam triggers smoke detectors. Heat detectors should be used instead. Ensure extraction fans are working
Dust and construction: Building work generates dust that contaminates smoke detectors. Isolate zones during works and have detectors cleaned or replaced afterwards
Aerosols and vaping: Hairspray, deodorant, and vape clouds can trigger smoke detectors. Ensure these are not used near detectors — consider relocating detectors if this is a persistent issue

When the Fire Alarm Sounds
Always treat every activation as real
Even if you suspect a false alarm, follow the full evacuation procedure. Never assume it's a false alarm and ignore it. People die from complacency — not from evacuating unnecessarily.
Evacuate immediately — follow your emergency evacuation procedure. Use the nearest fire exit. Do not use lifts. Close doors behind you
Go to the assembly point — the designated meeting area outside the building. Fire wardens must account for all staff, visitors, and contractors
Call 999 if the alarm is genuine or if you cannot determine the cause. Give the address and state whether people are still inside
Do not re-enter the building until the fire service or your fire warden gives the all-clear
Investigating the cause: Only a trained person should investigate after the alarm sounds. Check the panel — it shows which zone activated. If a call point was used, check if it was genuine or accidental. If a detector activated, look for visible signs of fire
Specific Premises Guidance
Schools and nurseries: Fire drills must be conducted at least once per term (3 times/year). Staff must know the location of every call point. Children must never be left unsupervised during evacuation. Visitors must be briefed on the fire procedure at sign-in
Care homes and hospitals: Progressive horizontal evacuation (moving residents to a safe compartment, not outside) is standard. Staff must know their specific role. PEEP (Personal Emergency Evacuation Plans) required for every resident/patient with mobility issues
HMOs and shared accommodation: Common areas must have linked smoke/heat detection (Category L2 minimum). Fire doors on every room opening onto escape routes. Emergency lighting on escape routes and stairwells
Offices and retail: Fire wardens should be appointed for each floor or department. Visitor sign-in must be maintained for roll-call purposes. Staff should receive fire safety training annually
Warehouses and workshops: High ceilings require beam detectors or aspirating systems — standard point detectors may not be effective. Flammable materials storage needs enhanced detection. Consult {{ company_name }} for specialist design
Your Legal Responsibilities
The Responsible Person (usually the employer, building owner, or managing agent) must ensure the fire alarm system is properly maintained, tested weekly, and serviced by a competent company every 6 months
Fire risk assessment: Must be carried out and regularly reviewed. The fire alarm system category should match the risk assessment recommendations. If the assessment says L2, you need L2 — not L3
Regulatory Reform (Fire Safety) Order 2005: Failure to maintain an adequate fire detection system can result in prosecution, unlimited fines, and imprisonment for the Responsible Person
Fire Safety (England) Regulations 2022: Buildings over 11 metres must have annual fire door checks. Responsible Persons must provide fire safety instructions to residents in all multi-occupied residential buildings
Insurance: Most commercial insurance policies require a certificated fire alarm system maintained to BS 5839. Failure to maintain your system may invalidate your fire insurance. Provide your insurer with copies of service reports

Understanding Your Detectors
Optical smoke detectors: Use an infrared LED and sensor inside a chamber. When smoke enters, light scatters onto the sensor and triggers the alarm. Best for slow-burning, smouldering fires (furniture, wiring). Most common type in modern systems
Heat detectors (fixed temperature): Trigger when the temperature reaches a set threshold (typically 58°C). Used in kitchens, boiler rooms, garages — anywhere smoke detectors would false-alarm. They detect fire later than smoke detectors, so are not suitable for sleeping areas
Heat detectors (rate-of-rise): Trigger when temperature rises rapidly (typically 8-10°C per minute). Faster response than fixed temperature. Used in areas with fluctuating but not rapid temperature changes
Multi-sensor detectors: Combine optical smoke and heat sensing in one unit. The panel analyses both signals to make a more intelligent decision — significantly reducing false alarms while maintaining fast genuine detection. The premium choice for most applications
Carbon monoxide (fire) detectors: Detect CO produced by fires at a very early stage — often before visible smoke. Increasingly used in combination with optical detectors for enhanced early warning
Beam detectors: Used in large open spaces (warehouses, atria, churches) where point detectors on the ceiling would be too high to detect smoke. A transmitter and receiver span the room — smoke breaking the beam triggers the alarm
Aspirating systems (VESDA/FAAST): Actively draws air through a pipe network into a laser-based detection chamber. Extremely early detection — can identify pre-combustion particles before visible smoke develops. Used in data centres, clean rooms, heritage buildings, and high-value areas
Key Changes in BS 5839-1:2025
Heat detectors banned in sleeping areas: Smoke or multi-sensor detectors must now be used in all rooms where people sleep — including in L2 and L3 systems. Heat detectors alone are no longer acceptable for sleeping risk
L4 systems — lift shafts: New requirement for detection at the top of lift shafts in L4 category systems
Red cables: All mains power circuits dedicated to the fire alarm system must now use red-coloured cables and be clearly labelled
ARC signal timing: Fire signals must reach the Alarm Receiving Centre within 90 seconds. Fault signals must be detected within 3 minutes
Service window: 6-monthly servicing now has a permitted window of 5-7 months. Servicing outside this window is non-compliant
Modifications: Any modification to an existing system now requires a formal certificate of extension or modification
Handover documentation: Enhanced requirements for documentation provided at handover, including system design rationale, zone charts, and operating instructions
Emergency Lighting — Often Linked to Fire Alarms
Emergency lighting illuminates escape routes when the mains power fails. It is a separate system from the fire alarm but equally important — you cannot evacuate safely in the dark
Testing: Monthly functional test (briefly interrupt mains to check lights illuminate) and annual 3-hour duration test (lights must remain on for the full rated duration). Record all tests in the fire log book
Battery replacement: Emergency lighting batteries typically last 3-4 years. If a unit fails its duration test, the battery likely needs replacing
BS 5266-1: The standard for emergency lighting. Maintenance and testing requirements are similar to fire alarms — 6-monthly professional servicing is recommended
